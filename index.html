An undersea infrastructure study

Beneath every
click, a cable.

Roughly 500 submarine cables carry nearly all intercontinental internet traffic. This is a layered look at who owns them, when they were built, and how they connect — scroll to add layers, scroll back to remove them.

Layer 03 — Age

predates the iPhone.
By decades.

Node size now reflects age — older cables (some from the 1990s, still in service) grow larger and drift outward, while recent builds stay small and tight to center. The network has rings, like a tree.

Layer 04 — Length & Reach

Some span an ocean.
Some span a strait.

Connection lines now vary in thickness and brightness by cable length — the longest intercontinental routes glow brightest, while short regional links stay faint. Scale becomes visible, without a map.

+ length-weighted links added
Layer 05 — Fragility

Every cable can break.
Most eventually do.

Pulsing nodes mark cables with a known history of faults or repairs — anchor drags, seismic activity, sabotage. The pulse rate is illustrative here, but built to carry real incident data.

+ fragility markers added
